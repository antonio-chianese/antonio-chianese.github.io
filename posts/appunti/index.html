Tenses

Referring to the present

TenseFunctionExamplesNote
Present simple
  • Regular habit
  • Something always true
I see my friends at the weekend–
Present continuous
  • A plan
  • A temporary situation
  • With always/forever
I’m seeing my friends at the weekendUsed with active verbs (not stative verbs, except those who calls back to an active verb, eg. having launch)
Present perfect
  • Simple:
    • To refer to the present result of a past action/event
    • To refer to an event/action that happened at an unspecified time in a period up to now
    • To focus on the number of times an action has been repeated
  • Continuous: An action that started in the past and has continues until the present
  • I’ve written the article (It’s finished)
  • I’ve been writing the article all morning (It probably isn’t finished)
  • Simple: Focuses on a completed action
  • Continuous: Focuses on an ongoning action

Referring to the past

TenseFunctionExamplesNote
Past simplePast events/actions/habits, with a time referenceI always watched the TV news when I lived abroad–
Past continuous
  • To show a continued action which was happening when another action took place
  • To refer to 2 actions happening at the same time in the past
  • The magazine was launched just when the sales of news magazines were falling
  • While some journalists were discussing the latest developments, others were watching the breaking news online
–
Past perfectTo show that an action happened earlier than another past actionI didn’t watch the programme about hurricanes on TV last night, because I’d already seen a similar programme–
Past perfect continuous
  • To refer to an action that happened during a period leading up to another past action
  • To show how long an action continued until a certain point in the past
  • The newspaper had been losing so much money that the owner decided to close it down
  • The reporters had been following the film star for days before they were able to interview her
–
  • Used to/Didn’t use to + Infinitive
  • Would + Infinitive
  • To refer to repeated actions or habits in the past that are no longer the case
  • To refer to a past state (not Would)
  • Before digital cameras were invented, people used to take/would take photos on film
  • This used to be (Would be) a very quiet neighbourhood
Used to is not normally used with time expressions specifying the duration of the action:
  • I used to live in Edinburgh before I moved to London
  • I lived (Used to live) in Edinburgh for five years before I moved to London

Referring to the future

TenseFunctionExamplesNote
Future simple (will + Infinitive)
  • To predict the future
  • To express a decision that has just been made (usually by the speaker)
  • To express the speaker’s insistence on doing something
  • Aftershocks from yesterday’s earthquake will probably continue for several days
  • I know! We’ll go to the Science Museum
  • We will find somewhere better to live - I promise you
–
Going to + Infinitive
  • For decisions/intensions about the future
  • For predictions about the future based on evidence, or to refer to the outcome of a process that has already started
  • The owner of the local bookshop is going to invite a well-known author to give a talk
  • You drive much too fast. You’re going to have an accident one of these days
–
Present continuousTo refer to something already arrangedWe’re meating the journalist tomorrow morning–
Present simpleFor future events fixed by a timetable or schedule
  • The sun rises at 5.30 tomorrow
  • The next train is at 11 o’clock
–
Future continuous
  • For temporary actions in progress at a particular time in the future
  • To ask about the listener’s plans (often to lead on to a request)
  • This time next week, we’ll be flying to Brazil
  • Will you be going out this evening? If not, can I come around and see you?
–
Future perfect
  • For actions in a period up to a particular time in the future
  • For actions which will be completed before a particular time in the future
  • The oil spill will have caused a lot of damage by the time the flow from the ship can be stopped
  • I’ll have finished lunch long before you arrive
–
Future perfect continuousFor actions in a period up to a particular time in the future, emphasising the lenght of timeAt the beginning of next March, I’ll have been working in the same job for 17 years–

NOTE: In time and conditional clauses referring to the future will is not normally used to refer to the future. Instead, present tenses (simple, continuous and perfect) are used.
